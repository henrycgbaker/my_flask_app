Cluster Analysis - in depth segmentation

This is the second panel visualization of a k-means clustering analysis for the 2019 UK elections. The two figures here are designed for party political campaign managers and communications officials (of any party).
The identified clusters are as follows:

  • Likely older, rural populations. Features lower population densities and higher age demographics (more retirees). Voting behavior might vary but could lean towards traditional values.
  • Highly urban, young populations. High population density, younger demographic. Might show diverse voting behaviors with significant votes for progressive parties.
  • Suburbanfamilies. Moderate population density, balanced age distribution, and mixed voting results. Likely a battleground for multiple parties.
  • Specialized populations (such as university towns or industrial hubs). Could have unique characteristics like high numbers of full-time students or specific economic dependencies.
  • Mixed rural and urban characteristics but with distinct features perhaps linked to specific local issues or demographic quirks not captured fully by the general metrics.

Collectively, these panels offer a more nuanced understanding of the demographics of specific voting subpopulations and their relationship to constituencies' political outcomes.


This is intended to assist campaign strategists and political stakeholders, by providing insights for more precise targeting of political messaging by segmenting political groups.


By segmenting clusters based on demographic dimensions, we gain insight into the disparities between communities and their differing political preferences, even those which seem similar along a given dimension(s):
for example, constituencies with low population density represent the norm, and vote for both the conservatives and Labour. However, understanding that in this constituencies with low population density, those that vote for the Conservatives primarily consist of older individuals in rural or suburban areas enables tailored messaging. Conversely, labor-voting constituencies in similarly low-population areas are almost completely devoid of older rural cluster, but instead exhibit a much more diverse mix of characteristics, as well as younger voters.


All this allows campaigners to target their messaging accordingly.


These two plots show mostly similar stories, but the second is more geared towards understanding the relative sizes of the clustered constituencies (and thus their political significance), whereas the scatter plot is more geared towards how these clusters then voted.
